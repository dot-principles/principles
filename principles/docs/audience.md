# DOC-AUDIENCE — Write for a specific, named audience

**Layer:** 2 (contextual)
**Categories:** documentation, readability
**Applies-to:** docs

## Principle

Every document should be written for a specific, identifiable reader with a known level of existing knowledge and a concrete goal. Before writing, answer: "Who is this for? What do they already know? What do they need to accomplish?" Write as if that person is reading over your shoulder. Assume background knowledge that your audience actually has; explain concepts they do not.

A document written for "anyone" is optimized for no one. Choosing an audience forces clarity about what to include, what to skip, and what vocabulary to use.

## Why it matters

When a document lacks a defined audience, authors hedge — they over-explain concepts experts find patronizing, while glossing over assumptions that beginners need spelled out. The result is a document that neither audience finds useful. Defining the audience up front makes every writing decision easier and every resulting sentence more useful.

## Violations to detect

- Documentation that alternates between beginner explanations and expert shortcuts in the same section
- Unexplained jargon in a getting-started guide targeted at newcomers
- Verbose background in reference material targeted at experienced users
- No stated audience or prerequisite knowledge at the top of a guide
- A single document covering both quick-start and advanced configuration without clear audience segmentation

## Good practice

- State the target audience and prerequisite knowledge in the first paragraph or a "Before you begin" section
- If multiple audiences need the same topic, write separate documents or clearly labeled sections
- Use the audience's vocabulary, not internal jargon, unless you define it on first use
- Test the document with a representative reader from the intended audience before publishing
- Link to prerequisite reading rather than duplicating background material
